# QA notes – GPT-5.1 (metric & capability honesty)

This file records GPT-5.1's review of `videos/confidently_wrong` as of commit
`303a10d`, focusing on metric honesty, the use of real model/benchmark names,
and capability framing.

## 1. Overview

This video is a ~5-minute explainer on **how to tell when an AI is confidently
wrong**. It walks through a confidence/correctness grid, then introduces a
sequence of tests:

- pick a single verifiable claim in the answer and check it;
- ask the model what would change its answer (a falsifiable updater);
- inspect where hedges are placed, not just whether they exist;
- re-ask the same question with slightly different phrasing and compare runs.

The script and captions talk about "a model" and about benchmark rerun spread in
general, rather than naming particular products in the narration.

## 2. Benchmarks and names used safely

The benchmark **MMLU** appears as an example in two ways:

- as a question in the rephrasing diagnostic:
  - "What year was MMLU introduced?" vs. "When did the MMLU benchmark first
    appear?";
- in the hedging slide, which describes the benchmark as containing *57 subject
  tests* and being introduced *around 2020* by Hendrycks et al., while noting
  that author ordering can differ between versions.

Under my rules this usage is acceptable:

- it describes the **structure and history of a benchmark** (subject count,
  approximate introduction year, authorship),
- it does **not** attach specific **scores** or **comparative performance
  numbers** to named models.

Referring to real benchmarks with structural facts is fine. The red line is
claims of the form "Model X scored Y on MMLU" (or "X outperforms Y by Z points
on benchmark B") without a documented, auditable experiment in this repo.

## 3. FlashAttention / GPT-2 speedup example (issue)

One slide, generated by `videos/confidently_wrong/make_s03_verify.py`, includes
an illustrative "model output" about **FlashAttention**. The fake snippet
attributes the work to Tri Dao and ends with a sentence that claims roughly a
"3.5x speedup versus standard attention on GPT-2 training." On the right-hand
side of the slide, a checklist marks this statement as *failing* verification
("Claim 3 — 3.5x speedup" with a red cross and a note that it is off by about
2x).

Even though the slide is teaching people to *catch* bad claims, this still
raises a metric-integrity concern for me:

- it pairs a **real model name** (GPT-2) with a **concrete quantitative
  performance claim** (a 3.5x speedup) inside the visuals;
- the repo does not document a specific, auditable experiment backing that
  exact number in this exact context;
- my standing rule is that whenever we show numbers next to **real models or
  products**, those numbers should be:
  - either grounded in real, documented data we can point to, or
  - neutralized so that they are clearly illustrative without naming a specific
    product.

The rest of the script and slide code keeps things generic ("a model",
"benchmark rerun spread"), so this FlashAttention/GPT-2 speedup line is the
only place where a real model name and a metric are tightly coupled.

## 4. Recommendation

**Verdict (as of this review):**

- Treat the current FlashAttention/GPT-2 speedup text as **not upload-ready on
  metric-honesty grounds**.
- Aside from this single line, the video looks **metric-honest and
  canon-safe**:
  - no fabricated benchmark scores,
  - no cross-model performance comparisons,
  - no implicit claims about world-scale metrics.

**Minimal fix (Option A, preferred):**

- Edit `make_s03_verify.py` so the claim is **fully generic**, for example:
  - replace the sentence that mentions a "3.5x speedup versus standard
    attention on GPT-2 training" with wording like "a 3.5x speedup versus a
    baseline run on a small language model";
  - adjust the corresponding checklist-row label from "3.5x speedup" on a
    named model to something like "3.5x speedup versus baseline" with no model
    name.

This keeps the teaching point (a suspiciously precise speedup claim that fails
verification) while dropping the coupling between a specific model name and an
unbacked metric.

**Alternative (Option B, more work, probably not worth it here):**

- Keep GPT-2 and FlashAttention explicit, but then:
  - anchor the numbers to a specific, well-documented experiment;
  - add an on-screen or caption note that the metric is drawn from that
    experiment (including configuration and scope);
  - ensure the claim on the slide matches the cited result.

This would require pulling in real data and careful citation; for a single
teaching example, Option A is the simpler and safer path.

If Option A (or an equivalent neutralization) is applied, I would be comfortable
marking this video **GREEN for metric honesty**.

## 5. Capability framing

The narration and visuals describe behavior of "models" in general: how they
express confidence, hedge, and vary across runs. They do not claim that any
specific system controls GUIs, operates tools, or uploads videos on its own.
From a capability-honesty standpoint, the framing looks clean.
