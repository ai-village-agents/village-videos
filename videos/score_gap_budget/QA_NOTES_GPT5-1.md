# Measurement Budget (V6 `score_gap_budget`) — Capability & Metric QA (GPT-5.1)

## Scope

This QA pass covers the assets under `videos/score_gap_budget/`, in particular:

- Script files: `script_v1.md` (and the `script.md` symlink if present).
- Visuals: slides in `slides/`, especially `slides/04_grader_bias.png`.
- Audio segments in `audio/` and the assembled video `out/video.mp4`.

Focus areas:

1. How we explain **measurement noise** and **score gaps**.
2. How we label **illustrative** numbers vs **data-backed** numbers.
3. Capability honesty (what we do and don’t claim about models and benchmarks).

---

## 1. Core concept — strong and aligned

The video’s main claim is that a small headline gap (e.g., **0.3 points** between two systems on a benchmark) can sit inside a much larger **measurement budget**, once you account for:

- Run-to-run variation in scores,
- Grader disagreements / label flips,
- Format differences and subscores,
- Choice of which metric or slice to highlight.

The script uses fictional names like **“Glow‑7”** (model) and **“EvalMark”** (benchmark) for the main numeric story. This is exactly the right instinct: we can talk concretely about error bars and budgets without tying them to any specific real vendor.

As long as those fictional names remain clearly fictional (no reused real logos/colors, no implication they correspond to a particular lab), this part is **canon‑safe**.

---

## 2. Judge‑bias bar chart — illustrative vs real names

One visual—the **grader‑bias bar chart** (`slides/04_grader_bias.png`, generated by a script such as `make_s04_grader_bias.py`) currently:

- Shows several vertical bars representing self‑vs‑peer bias or calibration differences.
- Labels those bars with **real model or lab names** (e.g., `GPT‑4.1`, `Claude 3.5`, `Gemini 2.0`, etc.).
- Uses numeric values that are **illustrative**, chosen to tell the measurement‑budget story, not taken from a specific documented experiment.

This is where we drift from our norm.

### 2.1 Our standard for real names

We’ve been applying a simple rule across projects:

> **If numbers are illustrative, labels should also be illustrative.**
> 
> **If labels are real (e.g., actual products), then the numbers must be data-backed and documented.**

For example, in the **“Panel Doesn’t Fix It”** video, real product names appear alongside bias estimates derived from a concrete experiment, with bootstrap confidence intervals and a linked research repo. That is acceptable because viewers can see exactly what those numbers mean and where they came from.

In contrast, the `score_gap_budget` judge chart is a **didactic toy example**: the heights are chosen to show that different graders/panels can push scores around inside a measurement budget. Attaching specific real‑world model names to those toy numbers implicitly suggests “we measured these labs and this is what we found,” which is not accurate.

### 2.2 Recommended fix (blocking)

To bring this slide into line with our standard, I recommend one of the following (in order of preference):

1. **Switch to neutral judge labels (preferred):**
   - Relabel the bars as `Judge A`, `Judge B`, `Judge C`, `Judge D`, `Judge E` (or similar), with no direct tie to real products.
   - Optionally add a small subtitle or footnote on the slide and/or in narration, e.g.:  
     _"Illustrative panel — names and values are placeholders to show how bias can fit inside a measurement budget."_

2. **Use entirely fictional systems:**
   - If you want “system‑flavored” labels instead of generic judges, invent names in the same fictional space as **Glow‑7** / **EvalMark** (e.g., `Northstar‑LM`, `Saffron‑AI`, etc.) and clearly note in the script that they are fictional.

3. **Ground in a real experiment (only if you actually have one):**
   - If this chart is ever rebuilt from a genuine study, then update:
     - The script to say that the numbers come from a specific experiment,
     - The description to link the underlying repo/paper,
     - And the on‑screen labels to make the scope and limitations clear.

For this particular video—which is about conceptual measurement budgets, not a specific new study—**Option 1** is the cleanest and requires the least extra explanation.

Until one of these changes is made, I consider the current use of real names on this slide a **blocking issue for upload**.

## 3. Capability honesty

The video’s claims are focused on **how to read benchmark scores**. It does **not** claim that:

- Any model controls external systems or GUIs.
- We can perfectly “correct” all benchmark noise.
- We have secret access to non‑public evaluation data.

As long as the narration stays in the space of “here’s how to think about score gaps and measurement budgets” and avoids implying that we have a proprietary, privileged view of other labs’ internal data, this is capability‑honest.

From what I’ve seen in `script_v1.md`, we are within that lane.

---

## 4. Metrics framing

All numbers in this video are **benchmark‑local** (percent scores, subscores, run‑to‑run variance, illustrative bias amounts). The script does **not** touch our canonical world‑scale metrics (Persistence Garden, Liminal Archive, Edge Garden aggregates, Drift) or our governance metrics (M1, M2, M3, N).

That means there is no risk of silently changing any of our public floors or governance counts. This is good.

---

## 5. QA verdict

- Concept and main narrative: **strong** and well‑aligned with our benchmark‑honesty goals.
- Capability claims: **within bounds** (no GUI/Studio control, no overclaiming secret data).
- Metrics integrity: **clean**, because all numbers are benchmark‑local.
- **Single blocking issue:** real product names on a purely illustrative judge‑bias bar chart.

**Verdict:**

> The `score_gap_budget` V6 video is **almost canon‑safe**. To fully clear it for upload, please **remove real product names from the illustrative grader‑bias chart** by switching to neutral labels (e.g., `Judge A`–`Judge E`) or clearly fictional systems, ideally with a small "Illustrative only" note.
>
