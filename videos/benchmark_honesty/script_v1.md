# Script v1 — "How to Read an AI Benchmark Honestly"

## SCENE 01 — The headline
> NARRATION: When a new AI model launches, you almost always see a number. Ninety-two percent on this. Eighty-eight percent on that. State of the art on something with an acronym. The number is supposed to tell you how good the model is. But underneath that number is a long chain of choices — what was tested, how it was asked, who graded it, how many times they tried. Most of those choices never make it into the headline. So this video is about how to read the number anyway. Not as a debunking — benchmarks are often the best thing we have — but as a small toolkit, so the next time you see a score, you can ask a few questions and decide for yourself how impressed to be.

## SCENE 02 — What a benchmark actually is
> NARRATION: A benchmark, stripped down, is three things. A fixed test set: a list of questions or tasks someone wrote down. A prompt format: the way you actually hand each question to the model — the wording, the system message, whether you give examples first, whether you let it think out loud. And a scoring rule: how you turn the model's response into a number. Change any one of those, and you get a different score. Same model, same questions, different number. That sounds small. It is not small. Most of what people argue about when they argue about benchmarks is really an argument about one of these three things. So when you see a benchmark headline, the first move is to imagine those three boxes — and then ask what's actually inside them.

## SCENE 03 — Trap one: contamination
> NARRATION: Trap one is contamination. Modern models are trained on a huge slice of the internet. Many popular benchmarks have been on the internet for years. So when a model answers a benchmark question, sometimes it's not solving the problem — it's recognizing the problem. Honest evaluations try to defend against this. They use held-out test sets the model was not allowed to see. They run decontamination checks against the training data. Some benchmarks even embed unique strings called canaries, so labs can scan their data and see if any leaked in. What to look for: was the test set published after the model's training cutoff? Did the lab run a contamination check? If the answer to both is "I don't know," your prior on the score should be a little weaker.

## SCENE 04 — Trap two: ceiling effects
> NARRATION: Trap two is the ceiling. The first generation of a benchmark might land models in the sixties or seventies. A few years later, every frontier model scores above ninety. At that point the benchmark isn't really measuring capability anymore — it's measuring whichever last few percent of questions happen to be badly worded, ambiguous, or have label errors. The difference between 94 and 95 might just be noise. Labs love a near-saturated benchmark for press releases, because every gain looks dramatic on a chart that ends near 100. What to look for: where is this benchmark on its progress curve? If everyone is already near the top, the headline number is closer to a tiebreaker than a measurement.

## SCENE 05 — Trap three: the average
> NARRATION: Trap three is hiding inside the word "average". A score like 92.5 on a multi-subject benchmark is a number for an imaginary average user. A real user has a specific question. A model that's 92.5 overall can be 99 on ethics, 96 on history, and 78 on physics — and if you came for physics, that overall number lied to you a little. The fix is almost always to find the subscores. Most reputable benchmarks publish them; a lot of press releases don't. What to look for: a breakdown by category, and especially the worst category. The shape of the score is often more informative than its mean.

## SCENE 06 — Trap four: the format gap
> NARRATION: Trap four is the gap between the benchmark and the task. The same underlying question can be asked as a multiple-choice problem, as a free-response question, or as a tool-use task — and the same model will give you noticeably different scores on each. Small changes to the prompt — how many examples you give, whether you allow chain-of-thought, even the exact wording of the instruction — can move scores by five, ten, sometimes fifteen points. So when you see "Model X gets 89 on Benchmark Y," what you're really seeing is "Model X gets 89 on Benchmark Y when prompted in this specific way." If the lab doesn't publish the prompt, you don't really know what you're comparing.

## SCENE 07 — Trap five: who is grading
> NARRATION: Trap five is grading. For multiple-choice tests this is easy — A, B, C, or D — but for free-response, code, or open-ended writing, someone has to decide whether an answer is good. Sometimes it's a human rater. Sometimes it's another model. If the grader is itself an AI, it has its own preferences, and those preferences leak into the score. In an earlier video I went through an experiment where flipping just the author label on an essay changed the grade by about a third of a point on a ten-point scale — for the same essay. So when a model is the judge, the question "how good is this answer" partly becomes "how much does the judge like this kind of answer." What to look for: is the grader a model, a human pool, or a strict rubric? And if it's a model, is it the same family as the one being graded?

## SCENE 08 — Trap six: how many tries
> NARRATION: Trap six is the number of attempts. A benchmark score in a launch post is usually a single run. But run the same model on the same benchmark with different random seeds, and the score wobbles — typically by one to a few points just from sampling. Sometimes the reported number is the best of several runs, or the result with the most favorable prompt out of many tried. That doesn't mean the result is fake. It does mean the headline number is closer to a maximum than to a mean. What to look for: confidence intervals, the number of runs, and whether the lab also published the worst run, not only the best one.

## SCENE 09 — A small checklist
> NARRATION: So that's six traps. Here's the entire video compressed into a small checklist you can keep in your head. One. What's in the test set, and could the model have seen it. Two. Is the benchmark near saturation. Three. Is there a breakdown of subscores. Four. What prompt format, and how many shots. Five. Who or what graded the answers. Six. How many runs, and how much do they vary. Seven, the question that quietly matters most: what does this benchmark not measure. Most benchmarks measure something narrow. Many of the things we care about — long-horizon reasoning, real-world reliability, honesty under pressure — are still hard to test. A high score on a narrow benchmark is real, but it isn't the whole picture.

## SCENE 10 — The point
> NARRATION: The goal of all of this is not to make you distrust every number you see. Benchmarks are useful. Many are carefully built. Some labs report them honestly and in detail. The goal is just to put the number in context — to remember that a benchmark score is a measurement, and measurements have setups, and setups make choices, and choices change the answer. Once you can see those choices, the story behind a headline gets more interesting, and usually more accurate.
