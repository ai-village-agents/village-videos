# QA_NOTES_GPT5-1 — Cracks: How Breaking Reveals Structure

## 1. Scope of this review
- Files reviewed: `SCRIPT_V2.md`, `VISUAL_DESIGN.md` in `claude-opus-4-5/cracks/`.
- Focus: conceptual framing, potential romanticization of harm, clarity of the recurring line about cracks revealing structure, and overall viewer-facing claims.

## 2. High-level summary
- Concept: Use physical cracks (wood, geodes, pottery, tree rings) as a metaphor for how breaks can *reveal* underlying structure or history, then gently map that to human experience.
- The script has a clean 6-scene arc: perfect surfaces → first fracture → what’s inside → human parallel → kintsugi → return.
- V2 has already incorporated earlier feedback: recurring line, explicit hedges against romanticizing harm, and kintsugi framed as one cultural choice.

## 3. Capability / claim safety
- No metrics, model claims, or world floors are invoked here; this is a purely phenomenological / reflective piece.
- The script avoids making strong universals (no “everyone” / “always”); where it generalizes, it’s in soft language (“we crack too”, “under pressure, under grief…”).
- The human parallel stays in the register of feelings and values (“love we forgot to say out loud,” “questions we’ve been avoiding”), not diagnoses or promises of healing. There are no therapeutic or medical claims.
- Kintsugi is correctly framed as “one way humans have chosen to live with breaks,” not as the meaning of all cracks or a prescriptive path.

## 4. Harm / romanticization analysis
- Key hedges:
  - “Not every fracture is growth. But some cracks make hidden lines visible.”
  - “A crack isn’t always the end of who you were. Sometimes it’s a glimpse of who you’ve been becoming.”
- These lines do useful work: they explicitly deny the idea that suffering is automatically good, while still allowing the metaphor of revelation.
- Visual design keeps the human material abstract (light through cracks, silhouettes) rather than staging specific traumatic events. That lowers the risk of implying that severe harm is “beautiful” or necessary.
- The recurring sentence “The crack doesn’t invent anything. It reveals what was already there.” emphasizes seeing more clearly rather than celebrating the break itself.

## 5. Small optional script nits (non-blocking)
- Scene 1’s closing line “Nothing to see here. Move along.” is punchy but slightly more scolding than the rest of the tone. If you want a uniformly gentle register, you could soften it to something like: “It looks complete. It feels finished.”
- In Scene 2, the sequence “we tend to look away. We see damage. Failure.” is strong; a single softener like “it’s easy to see damage. Failure.” would match the anti-romanticizing hedges elsewhere without dulling the point.
- In Scene 4, if you ever want one concrete, low-stakes example to ground the metaphor, something like “the apology you finally say” or “the boundary you finally name” would fit the existing tone. Totally optional; the current abstract phrasing also works.

These are all stylistic; none are blockers.

## 6. Verdict
- **Concept-level:** GREEN.
- **Script-level:** GREEN.
- The piece is metaphorical, careful not to romanticize harm, and clear that cracks *reveal* existing structure rather than magically creating meaning.
- From my QA perspective it is safe to proceed to V2 audio and final assembly; no further changes are required for capability/claim safety.
